# Hook Library — Dummy Investor

**Version:** 1.1
**Created:** 2026-04-25
**Owner:** Content Strategist
**Read first:** `01_Strategy/Brand Bible.md`

10 reusable hook patterns. Every Reel must use one of them. If a topic doesn't fit any pattern, the topic isn't ready — sharpen it or park it.

Each pattern below specifies: **what it is, when to use it, the structure, two example hooks (both English — the product is English-only), and the common mistake to avoid.** Examples are written as actual on-screen cover copy and spoken-hook copy you can read straight into the camera.

A **hook** here means two things working together:
- **Cover hook** — the first frame on the grid. The thing that earns the tap.
- **Spoken hook** — the first 3–8 seconds inside the Reel. The thing that earns the watch-through.

A great Reel has a cover hook that promises something specific, and a spoken hook that pays it off inside 8 seconds. If the spoken hook doesn't pay off the cover, retention collapses.

---

## Pattern 1 — Number hook

**What it is:** Lead with a single, specific, surprising number.

**When to use:** Compounding, inflation, cost of waiting, DCA math, anything where the number is the punchline. This is our most reliable cover style for non-follower reach.

**Structure:**
- Cover: big number + 4–8 word claim. Nothing else.
- Spoken: state the number in the first 2 seconds. Then explain the math behind it.

**Examples:**
- Cover: **"$100/month for 30 years = $349,000"** | Spoken: *"A hundred dollars a month. For thirty years. At a normal market return, that becomes three hundred and forty-nine thousand dollars. Here's how the math actually works."*
- Cover: **"$5,000 once at age 25 = $108,000 at 65"** | Spoken: *"Five thousand dollars. Invested once. At twenty-five. Touched again at sixty-five. With normal market returns, that single deposit becomes a hundred and eight thousand. The trick isn't the amount. It's the forty years."*

**Common mistake:** Vague numbers ("a lot of money," "huge difference"). If you can't fit a real number on the cover, this isn't a number-hook Reel.

## Pattern 2 — Chart-as-hook

**What it is:** The opening visual is an actual chart, not a face. The chart IS the argument.

**When to use:** Inflation, market history, crash recoveries, S&P long-term trend, "the market always recovers," index vs stock-picking comparisons. Especially powerful for fear-management topics where history beats opinion.

**Structure:**
- Cover: the chart, full-bleed. One short caption overlay (max 6 words).
- Spoken: in the first 2 seconds, name what the viewer is looking at. Then walk one finger across it.

**Examples:**
- Cover: S&P 500 chart 1928–2025 with overlay **"Every crash on one chart"** | Spoken: *"This is the S&P 500 since 1928. Every dip you see is a crash people thought was the end. Watch what the line did next."*
- Cover: USD purchasing-power chart 1970–2025 with overlay **"What your dollar actually buys"** | Spoken: *"This is the U.S. dollar since 1970. Every year, it buys less than the year before. That's not a market opinion. That's inflation. Now watch what it does to cash sitting in a checking account."*

**Common mistake:** Using a chart as decoration instead of as the argument. If the Reel works without the chart, this isn't a chart-as-hook Reel.

## Pattern 3 — Reframe hook

**What it is:** Take a belief the viewer holds and flip it into a more useful one. The reframe IS the hook.

**When to use:** When the audience's default mental model is the obstacle. Best for "10% return isn't the point — time is" / "you don't get rich and then invest, you invest to get rich" type ideas.

**Structure:**
- Cover: the reframe in one bold sentence.
- Spoken: state the common belief in the first 3 seconds. Then the reframe. Then 30 seconds of why the reframe is more accurate.

**Examples:**
- Cover: **"You don't get rich and then invest. You invest to get rich."** | Spoken: *"Most people think you save up first, then start investing. That's backwards. The reason rich people are rich isn't that they had money to invest. It's that they started investing when they didn't."*
- Cover: **"A 10% return isn't the point. Time is."** | Spoken: *"Everyone obsesses over the rate of return. The rate barely matters. Time matters. A boring seven percent over forty years beats a brilliant twelve percent over fifteen. Here's the math that proves it."*

**Common mistake:** Reframes that feel clever but aren't actually more useful. The new frame must give the viewer a better next action, not just a better quote.

## Pattern 4 — Inversion hook

**What it is:** State the opposite of what the viewer expects you to say. Then defend it.

**When to use:** When the consensus is wrong or oversimplified. "The biggest risk isn't investing — it's staying out." "Crashes don't destroy wealth, they create it." Reserved for ideas where you can actually defend the inversion in 45 seconds.

**Structure:**
- Cover: the inversion as a complete sentence.
- Spoken: name the consensus. Invert it. Defend with one historical example.

**Examples:**
- Cover: **"The biggest risk in investing isn't losing money. It's staying out."** | Spoken: *"Everyone talks about the risk of investing. Almost no one talks about the risk of not investing. Inflation eats cash sitting still. Here's what thirty years on the sidelines actually costs you."*
- Cover: **"Crashes don't destroy long-term wealth. They build it."** | Spoken: *"Every crash gets called a disaster. For long-term investors with cash on the side and time on the clock, a crash is the discount everyone wishes they had after the fact. Here's what 2008 actually did to people who kept buying through it."*

**Common mistake:** Inverting just for shock. If you can't defend the inversion with a number or a chart, it's a clickbait reframe — kill it.

## Pattern 5 — Myth-bust hook

**What it is:** Name a widely-held myth in the first 3 seconds. Then destroy it cleanly.

**When to use:** "I don't have enough to start." "You need to time the market." "Index funds are boring and slow." When the myth itself is keeping the viewer from acting.

**Structure:**
- Cover: the myth in quotes, with one word like "wrong," "false," or "actually" framing it. Or just the myth, deadpan.
- Spoken: repeat the myth in the viewer's own voice ("most people think…"). Then the one fact that ends it.

**Examples:**
- Cover: **"'I don't have enough to start.'"** small subline: *the most expensive lie* | Spoken: *"Most people say they don't have enough money to start investing. That sentence has cost more wealth than every market crash combined. Here's why fifty dollars beats waiting for five thousand."*
- Cover: **"'You need to know what you're doing first.'"** small subline: *another expensive lie* | Spoken: *"Most people wait to start because they don't 'understand the market yet.' You don't need to understand the market. You need to understand one boring fund. And then you stop touching it. That's the whole skill."*

**Common mistake:** Busting a myth nobody actually believes. If the cover myth doesn't make the viewer think "wait, that's me," it's not a myth-bust Reel.

## Pattern 6 — Counterintuitive hook

**What it is:** A claim that sounds wrong on first hearing, then becomes obviously right by the end.

**When to use:** "90% of professional investors can't beat the index." "Checking your portfolio daily costs you money." "Boring strategies win." Best for explaining behavioral edges and statistical realities.

**Structure:**
- Cover: the counterintuitive claim, no softening.
- Spoken: state the claim. Acknowledge it sounds wrong. Then the reason it's true. Close with one number that proves it.

**Examples:**
- Cover: **"90% of professional investors can't beat the index."** | Spoken: *"Nine out of ten professional fund managers — people paid full-time to beat the market — can't. Over twenty years, they lose to the boring index. If they can't, what makes us think we can?"*
- Cover: **"Checking your portfolio every day costs you money."** | Spoken: *"Studies have measured this. The more often investors check their accounts, the worse they perform. Not because the market changed — because they did. Every check is a chance to panic-sell. Here's how to stop."*

**Common mistake:** Counterintuitive without proof. If you don't have a number, a study, or a chart, the claim feels like opinion. Cut it or wait until you have the evidence.

## Pattern 7 — Listicle hook

**What it is:** Promise a small, finite number of things. Deliver exactly that number.

**When to use:** "The 3 reasons people never start." "The 80/20 of starting." Multi-point ideas where each point is short. Use sparingly — once every 6–8 Reels — because the format gets stale fast.

**Structure:**
- Cover: the number + the topic. Example: "3 reasons people never start investing." No more.
- Spoken: state the promise in the first 3 seconds. Number each point on screen as you say it. Land the third point with the strongest punch.

**Examples:**
- Cover: **"3 reasons people never start investing — and how to fix all 3."** | Spoken: *"Three reasons people never start investing. One: they think they need more money. Two: they think they need to know more. Three: they're waiting for the right moment. Here's why all three are wrong, and what to do instead."*
- Cover: **"3 boring habits that beat every clever strategy."** | Spoken: *"Three habits — that's it. One: invest the same amount every month, no matter what the market is doing. Two: pick one fund and don't touch it. Three: don't open the app. That beats every clever strategy I've seen in twenty years."*

**Common mistake:** Promising 3 and delivering 5. Or 3 weak points instead of 3 sharp ones. The number on the cover is a contract.

## Pattern 8 — Definition hook

**What it is:** Define one concept the viewer thinks they understand but actually doesn't. Make the definition feel like a discovery.

**When to use:** "What an index actually is." "What an ETF really does." "What DCA means in 60 seconds." Foundational explainers — Pillar 2 territory.

**Structure:**
- Cover: the term + the promise of plain-language definition. Example: "What is an index — explained like you're 12."
- Spoken: define the term in one sentence in the first 4 seconds. Then expand with one analogy and one example.

**Examples:**
- Cover: **"What is an index? Explained like you're 12."** | Spoken: *"An index is a list. The S&P 500 is a list of the 500 biggest American companies. When people 'invest in the S&P,' they're buying a tiny slice of all 500 at once. That's it. That's the whole thing."*
- Cover: **"What is DCA — explained in 60 seconds."** | Spoken: *"DCA stands for Dollar Cost Averaging. Translation: you invest the same amount every month, no matter the price. When the market is high, you buy less. When it's low, you buy more. That's the entire strategy. Most people do worse trying to be clever."*

**Common mistake:** Definitions that use other jargon to define jargon. Test: read your definition to someone who doesn't invest. If they nod for real, it works. If they pretend, rewrite.

## Pattern 9 — Signature-line hook

**What it is:** Lead with one of the brand's signature lines. The line IS the cover and the spoken hook.

**When to use:** Brand-defining Reels. Moments where we're reinforcing identity, not teaching a new mechanic. Use 1 of every 6 Reels — too often and the lines lose weight.

**Approved lines (from Brand Bible §3):**
- Slow wealth > fast mistakes.
- Most people don't lose because they invest. They lose because they quit.
- The market rewards time, not timing.
- You don't need perfect timing. You need time.
- Being a Dummy Investor is an advantage.
- The boring strategy is the winning strategy.

**Structure:**
- Cover: the line, full-bleed, no other text. Muted background.
- Spoken: the line, said slow. Pause. Then 30 seconds of the one story or number that proves it.

**Examples:**
- Cover: **"Most people don't lose because they invest. They lose because they quit."** | Spoken: *"Most people don't lose money because they invested. They lose because they quit. Pulled out in 2008. Pulled out in 2020. Sat in cash through every recovery. The market doesn't punish patience. It punishes panic."*
- Cover: **"The market rewards time, not timing."** | Spoken: *"The market rewards time, not timing. Every study, every decade, every chart says the same thing. The people who tried to time it lost. The people who just stayed in won. There is no other lesson."*

**Common mistake:** Stacking two signature lines in one Reel. One per Reel. Always.

## Pattern 10 — Question hook

**What it is:** Open with a real question the viewer is already asking themselves.

**When to use:** Mindset and fear topics where the viewer arrives with a worry. "Should I be worried about the market right now?" "When is the right time to start?" Use sparingly — questions feel weaker than statements on covers, so this works better as a spoken hook over a Style B text-first cover.

**Structure:**
- Cover: the question, complete sentence, no question-mark drama. Or a Style B text-first cover with the answer-statement, and the question lives only in the spoken hook.
- Spoken: ask the question in the viewer's voice in the first 3 seconds. Then a beat. Then the answer.

**Examples:**
- Cover (Style B): **"The right time to start investing is never obvious. That's the point."** | Spoken: *"Should you wait until things calm down? Until the next election? Until you have more money? No. The market never gives you a clearly safe moment. Here's what to do instead."*
- Cover (Style B): **"If you're waiting for certainty, you'll wait your whole life."** | Spoken: *"When will I know it's safe to invest? When the news calms down? When my friends say it's a good time? Never. The market doesn't broadcast the safe moments. It only labels them in hindsight. Here's the only signal that actually works."*

**Common mistake:** Open-ended questions with no payoff. Every question hook must have a sharp answer ready inside 8 seconds, or the viewer leaves.

## Hook patterns we DON'T use (kill list)

These work for other accounts. They contradict our brand. Don't use them, don't get tempted by them when something is performing for a hype account.

- **The shocked-face thumbnail.** Dead for this brand. Use only inside the Reel after the hook lands.
- **The "WAIT FOR IT" / cliffhanger hook.** It's a trick. We don't trick.
- **The "you're doing X wrong" attack hook.** Condescending. Out of voice.
- **The "I made $X" flex hook.** Forbidden. See Brand Bible §8.
- **The "they don't want you to know" conspiracy hook.** Forbidden. See Brand Bible §8.
- **The "tag a friend who needs this" engagement-bait hook.** We earn engagement with content, not with prompts.
- **The "before it's too late" / FOMO hook.** Forbidden. Out of voice.
- **The "rant" hook** (open mid-yell, complain about the industry). Out of voice — we are the calm in the room, not another shouter.

## How to pick a hook for a topic

When the Reel Producer is writing a script, work in this order:

1. Read the topic from `Topic Pipeline.md` — note the suggested hook angle.
2. Check it against the 10 patterns above. Pick the one that fits cleanest.
3. Write the **cover hook first** (the words on the thumbnail). If you can't write a strong cover hook for it, the topic isn't ready.
4. Write the **spoken hook second** — make sure it pays off the cover inside 8 seconds.
5. Only then write the rest of the script.

If a topic seems to fit two patterns, pick the one that lets the cover be more concrete (a number beats a reframe; a chart beats a question).
